One of the problems with explaining the Riemann Hypothesis is that its fascination comes from its deep connection to prime numbers, but its definition is in terms of complex analysis which requires a fair deal of undergraduate mathematics to understand -- and that is before you even got started to grasp what the heck the zeta-zeros have to do with the distribution of primes. My "cocktail party explanation" of the Riemann Hypothesis would usually be something like: "The prime numbers are as equally distributed as you could wish for." But there is actually a surprisingly easy interpretation of the Riemann Hypothesis: "Prime numbers behave like a random coin toss."<!--more-->

Let's first take a look at a regular (fair) coin, that is, the two outcomes "heads" or "tails" are equally likely. We now do an experiment: we start counting at <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_cfcd208495d565ef66e7dff9f98764da.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, and toss our coin repeated. Whenever we throw "heads", we add <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c4ca4238a0b923820dcc509a6f75849b.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> to our count, when it's a "tails", we subtract <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c4ca4238a0b923820dcc509a6f75849b.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. What will be the count after <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> coin tosses?

Mathematically speaking, we define a sequence of independent random variables <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_a97118fb9e8d7e006a466bfc0771f888.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> where

</p>

Now we are interested in what the sum is, i.e., we define

  <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_37c6fa1dca5134a629bab76d54d7d792.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>
</p>

Because of the <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_a97118fb9e8d7e006a466bfc0771f888.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>'s symmetry, they each have <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_4309f2278608b87a7fde0154e0c16dd4.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, and hence we also have <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_f36a75e3fbd79486a7746bc50a8fec8f.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> for all <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> (due to the linearity of the expectation value). In other word, if we were to bet on what our total count would be at any given point, we should put our money on <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_cfcd208495d565ef66e7dff9f98764da.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>.<sup id="rf1-597"><a href="#fn1-597" title="Of course, Y_n can actually only ever be 0 for even n." rel="footnote">1</a></sup>

However, reality looks different.

(You see why this process is known as a <a title="Random walk" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_walk" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">random walk</a>.) When we stopped at <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_e0f7519ba92c55d4377262f48695ab0d.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, we were off <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_cfcd208495d565ef66e7dff9f98764da.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> by about <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_08f90c1a417155361a5c4b8d297e0d78.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. After all, that shouldn't be too surprising. In theory, <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_6dd41b22f5a18d6950bfc58ca5ec10e2.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> could take any value between <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_9cefd96d40d0ee1256c8eff1def0312d.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> and <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. However, these boundary values would be extremely unlikely: the probability that <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_5764936b111df9d022667582e1688729.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> is indeed <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_1c4d4b01cdd685adb4b8cd6c789a797b.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, a ridiculously small number.

Still, despite its tendency to oscillate around the <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>-axis, we would observe that <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_6dd41b22f5a18d6950bfc58ca5ec10e2.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> would reach arbitrary distances from it as <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> grows larger and larger. The way to quantify this behaviour is to measure the _standard deviation_ of <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_6dd41b22f5a18d6950bfc58ca5ec10e2.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. I won't go into the details, but if you're familiar with these calculations you won't have a problem verifying that

</p>

This means that while we expect <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_6dd41b22f5a18d6950bfc58ca5ec10e2.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> to be <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_cfcd208495d565ef66e7dff9f98764da.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> on average, we would expect the values to spread up to <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_be183cdd1e68ce30a59b96233609b08f.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>:

More precisely, we would expect values between <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c7f202540694013cdad7316152484d6b.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> and <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_63d03ad3e1c937558db4a2863c22db84.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> in <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_9b8185bbd7878ce8b2469aecddf15ba6.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> of the cases if we repeated the experiment over and over again. But for random walks, we can make an even more precise statement. The <a title="Law of the iterated logarithms" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_the_iterated_logarithm" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">law of the iterated logarithm</a> asserts that the rate of growth of a random walk is governed by <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_4b04ce606bcf0e7a804d5c84f45b865a.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, so we might say<sup id="rf2-597"><a href="#fn2-597" title="The law of the iterative logarithm even gives us the value of the implicit constant that&nbsp;the big-O notation surpresses: it&#039;s the square root of 2." rel="footnote">2</a></sup>

</p>

which of course has to be interpreted with some good will<sup id="rf3-597"><a href="#fn3-597" title="That is, the number of exceptions will have probability 0." rel="footnote">3</a></sup> since we are talking here about a random sequence, not a deterministic one.

That's all very well, but what does it have to do with primes or the Riemann Hypothesis? To see this, we need to remember the Möbius function <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_dd212270229767191a8e42b95f2e9c33.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> that I [half-heartedly introduced some time ago][4]. The definition of this function looks very odd and artificial at first, but is actually a very natural and useful function in the theory of numbers, not least because of the Möbius inversion mentioned in the article above. We define <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_dd212270229767191a8e42b95f2e9c33.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> as follows: if <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> has <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_8ce4b16b22b58894aa86c421e8759df3.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> prime factors, all of which are distinct (i.e., <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> is squarefree), then <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_6922fa1d73485ff7f6a15d49f22ce108.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, otherwise, if <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> has at least one repeated prime factor, <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_a24459a83a063e0a362f0c4b81e866a2.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. The sequence <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_dd212270229767191a8e42b95f2e9c33.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> looks like this:

Notice how <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_e81f755b39d84826181bb0d1aa7a44f7.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> for primes <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_83878c91171338902e0fe0fb97a8c47a.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> since they have exactly one prime factor (themselves). But there are also composite values with <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_d1465ed7af7a8ed4743f4a768fd26541.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, e.g., <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_3286947544169f362c046c652a0682dc.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:1px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. In general, it seems pretty hard to predict what value will come next. The sequence looks _random_. Of course, it isn't actually random since the values are predefined by the arguments' prime factors. It is exactly this unpredictable, yet not erratic nature of the primes that the zeta-zeros encode.

To see what I mean, we'll sum up the values of <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_dd212270229767191a8e42b95f2e9c33.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> just like we did with out coin tosses. Since this is not actually a random variable, we cannot talk of expectation values or standard deviations, but we can control its rate of growth. If <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_505b30474bf75fb6f9f0d876bf9fdcf1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span><sup id="rf4-597"><a href="#fn4-597" title="We entered the realm of big O where constants don&#039;t matter. The zero values of the M&ouml;bius function&nbsp;only change the constant, and hence it doesn&#039;t matter if we consider the Mertens function with the zero values, or the prime walk without them." rel="footnote">4</a></sup> really behaved like a random walk, we would expect it to be delimited by the square root function, and indeed did Franz Mertens conjecture the stronger statement that <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_251247552b452a26038b35503365e9a0.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> for all <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>. This turned out to be wrong<sup id="rf5-597"><a href="#fn5-597" title="Though only the existence of a violation is known, but an&nbsp;actual value is beyond the scope of the current calculating power -- yet another example that you can&#039;t trust empirical evidence when it comes to primes." rel="footnote">5</a></sup>, but not all is lost -- after all, we don't have such a tight bound on our coin tosses either. Instead, we would be happy with something like

<p style='text-align:center;'>
  <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_adf04f828b4ce64372f5b6205f571d8a.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>
</p>

_This indeed is equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis._ To see this, we first observe that

<p style='text-align:center;'>
  <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_8317c2b517e3032b669e4d47e6bf1cfb.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>
</p>

(There are many clever ways to see this, but you will have to trust me on this -- or, much better, verify it yourself.) Through a clever sequence of transformations, we express <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_505b30474bf75fb6f9f0d876bf9fdcf1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> as an integral, and then get it out of it via Mellin transform to obtain

<p style='text-align:center;'>
  <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_2f1e49966b205b9ba71cfdb780cfc07b.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none;' class='tex' alt="" /></span>
</p>

The crucial question is for what values of <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_a2ab7d71a0f07f388ff823293c147d21.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> this is valid. We will change the value of the integral if we push the line of integration to the left past a singularity in the integrand. This will happen if there is a zero in the denominator. Voilà, the zeta-zeros entered the stage!

If Riemann was right, there will be no zeros for <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_f5cbe15106ab07bce1b9ced6752e62c2.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, and hence we could choose <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_a2ab7d71a0f07f388ff823293c147d21.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; padding-bottom:2px;' class='tex' alt="" /></span> to be as little as <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_075194ea49553b37188f56467a61329e.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> (where <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_59325a7cdca6b97119f9fae77546babd.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> is an arbitrarily small number). But then it is not difficult to convince yourself<sup id="rf6-597"><a href="#fn6-597" title="Just observe that x^(s+it)=O(x^s). Then all you need is a good bound for the denominator." rel="footnote">6</a></sup> that this implies <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_f848453a3ba189e8bfec7d7a68508a54.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>, just as we were hoping for.

In other words: if the Riemann Hypothesis is true, then the sequence <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_505b30474bf75fb6f9f0d876bf9fdcf1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span> is _very_ close (though not quite) to looking like a random walk. What's more is that the converse is true as well, i.e., you could deduce the Riemann Hypothesis from the above bound for <span class='MathJax_Preview'><img src='http://localhost:8885/riemannhypothesis.info/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_505b30474bf75fb6f9f0d876bf9fdcf1.gif' style='vertical-align: middle; border: none; ' class='tex' alt="" /></span>: if Riemann is false, then there are zeros off the critical line and we will know that the Mertens function has a wider spread than a random walk. That's why we say that these statements are equivalent.

So when someone asks you next time how it is to work on primes, just say that it's like flipping a coin -- you never know what you'll get.
